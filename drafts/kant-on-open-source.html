Kant on open-source software

Date Tue 15 November 2016 Tags opinion / tutorials

In one of my more recent posts, "Paying for programs in the Age of Free", I argued that software theft could be rejected based on a Kantian argument for self-actualization. In other words, stealing software is unethical because the thief is infringing upon the programmer's right to self-actualization, which is manifest in the programmer's request for compensation to use software. Since the theft is a violation of the programmer's intrinsic right to self-actualization, it should not be accepted.

If you spend enough time reading around, you'll find that philosophy as a field could be seen as very large, very old food fight. Lots of philosophers reject lots of other philosophers' arguments, dirty looks are forever being exchanged, and cocktail parties aren't generally found to be very much fun. People are still picking fights with Aristotle. This makes it sort of messy to keep track of everything, but it makes for entertaining reading. It also means that there are often dissimilar arguments supported by different readings (interpretations) of the same work.
